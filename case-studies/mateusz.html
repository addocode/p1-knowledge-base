Mateusz

Case Study·Updated 28 Jun 2026·7 min·Confidence: Medium

Mateusz is documented as a broker-case study: useful, active and investment-oriented, but also an example of how a professional’s preferred thesis can influence how alternatives are framed.

v6.0 Knowledge EditionCase Study

TL;DR

Case summary
  • Role: broker / intermediary in the Gdańsk search.
  • Strength: active sourcing, investment perspective and ability to present alternative theses.
  • Risk: strong preference for Młode Miasto/M15 may have caused Letnica alternatives to be framed too negatively.
  • Key lesson: broker opinions are useful hypotheses, not final evidence.
  • P1 rule: data and field observation must be weighted above sales narratives.

Role in P1

Mateusz contributed to the Gdańsk search by introducing objects, alternatives and investment narratives. After the Porto candidate disappeared, he shifted attention toward a Młode Miasto / ROBYG thesis. This was not necessarily dishonest; it may have reflected a genuine belief that the city-development asset had stronger long-term potential.

Narrative bias

The important learning is not that Mateusz was wrong. The learning is that every intermediary has a lens. Once a broker believes in one object or district, alternatives may be unconsciously weakened. Statements such as “no tourist comes here” or “students will not come here” should be treated as hypotheses and tested against data, not accepted as facts.

Data vs opinion

Remigiusz’s real Booking data and winter rental documentation contradicted overly absolute claims about Letnica demand. The P1 conclusion became simple: opinions are useful, but actual revenue records and direct field observation deserve higher weight.

Investor lesson

A broker can be both helpful and biased. The investor’s task is not to reject brokers, but to translate their statements into testable questions. Good brokers provide access and market texture; disciplined investors provide independent judgment.
